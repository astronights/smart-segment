Data

We create sample data to simulate the results of a binary classification / propensity model.

As common with unbalanced datasets, we create the dataframe skewed towards the negative class, with very few positive labels. As a result, the Gamma distribution is used to simulate these values.

The true labels y generated are meant to indicate the outcome obtained after the implementation period on the user conversions.

Optimization

The optimization algorithm aims to find the optimum number of splits as well as the boundaries for each split maximizing objective functions.

The model has its algorithm relying on the objective function, which requires the cost and revenue incurred for each user. This allows for providing cost and revenue functions using the index of the bin as a parameter. This was created with the idea that customers with higher probabilities might require more specialized communication material thus incurring a higher cost, and vice versa.

The revenue function used is a constant value for all customers: lambda ix: 30.

The cost function used depends on the bin index increasing monotonicallt: lambda ix: 2*ix.

A penalty factor can be used when the model is stuckon local minimas to push further exploration. In this example, we set it to 0.

The maximized equation is Revenue of Converted Customers - Cost of All Customers - Exploitation Penalty.

  • Cost function to determine the cost of marketing to each customer within that consumer group.
  • Revenue function to determine the revenue gained from the conversion of a customer within that consumer group.
  • Local minimum penalty factor (Penalty for not continuing exploration - used when results are not well distributed).

The optimization algorithm provides for several other configurable features:

  • Number of splits to include
  • Minimum number of samples per split
  • Search method (Global / Quantile based)

def plot_stats(bin_df: pd.DataFrame, title: str):
    'Plotting the statistics'

    # Group counts by bins
    group_df = bin_df.groupby('bin', observed=True)['y'].agg(['count', 'sum']).reset_index(names='bins')

    # Calculate bin widths, conversions and indices
    group_df['conversion'] = group_df['sum'] / group_df['count']
    group_df['bin_width'] = group_df['bins'].apply(lambda x: x.right - x.left)
    group_df['bin_index'] = range(1, len(group_df)+1)

    # Calculate revenue per bin - costs
    group_df['incremental'] = (group_df['sum'] * group_df['bin_index'].apply(revenue_fn) -
                               group_df['count'] * group_df['bin_index'].apply(cost_fn))
    group_df['revenue_per_capita'] = group_df['incremental'] / group_df['count']

    # Normalize values for colors
    c_min, c_max = group_df['conversion'].min(), group_df['conversion'].max()
    group_df['color'] = (group_df['conversion'] - c_min) / (c_max - c_min)

    # Create Plot
    plt.figure(figsize=(20,8))
    cmap = plt.cm.RdYlGn

    # Set up tick labels and widths
    ws = group_df['bin_width']
    bins = group_df['bins']
    x_ticks = [sum(ws[:i]) + ws[i]/2 for i in range(len(ws))]
    tick_labels = sorted(set([round(float(y), 2) for x in bins for y in (x.left, x.right)]))

    # PLot Data
    convert_col = group_df['conversion'].copy()
    bars = plt.bar(x_ticks, height = group_df['count'], width = ws, color = cmap(convert_col), alpha = 0.8)
    plt.plot(x_ticks, group_df['sum'], color='black', marker='o', linestyle='-', label='Conversions')

    for i, bar in enumerate(bars):
        plt.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2, bar.get_height() + 100, f'{round(group_df.revenue_per_capita.iloc[i], 1)}$', 
                 ha='center', va='bottom', fontsize=10, color='black')


    _ = plt.xticks(tick_labels, tick_labels, rotation=30, ha='right')
    plt.colorbar(plt.cm.ScalarMappable(cmap=cmap, norm=plt.Normalize(vmin=c_min, vmax=c_max)), ax=plt.gca(), label='Conversion Rate')
    plt.title(f'{title} Split - Conversion Rates & Revenue / Customer ($)')
    plt.tight_layout()

    plt.savefig(f'analyses/{title.lower().replace(" ", "_")}_plot.png', dpi=300, bbox_inches='tight')
    plt.show()

    return group_df

Insights

Here are a few interesting insights into how the Optimized method outperforms the Uniform and Percentile method.

Targeting High-Value Segments More Precisely

In the Optimized method, customers are more effectively grouped based on their likelihood to convert and their potential revenue. The smaller segments for customers with the highest conversion rates (e.g., $(0.915, 1.0]$) allow for more precise targeting. As a result:

The last three groups, representing only 121 customers combined, exhibit conversion rates above $89%$, with revenue per customer reaching $\$14$ to $\$15$. In contrast, both Uniform and Percentile methods have broader segments that dilute high-value customers within larger groups, leading to missed opportunities for maximizing revenue.

Balancing Volume and Revenue

The Optimized method ensures that even customers in lower segments (with lower conversion likelihood) still contribute positively to revenue. For example:

In the lowest segment $(0.0, 0.154]$, there are $45,550$ customers with a relatively low conversion rate of $9.12%$, but they still generate positive total revenue of $33,520$, with a modest revenue per customer of $\$0.73$. Compare this to the Uniform method, where large customer segments like $(0.0, 0.1]$ actually generate negative revenue $(-\$2780)$ due to insufficient targeting of high-potential customers, and similar issues are seen across the Percentile method with its first 8 groups having negative revenue per customer.

Maximizing High-Converting Groups

The Optimized segmentation approach clusters customers more intelligently in the middle conversion range, where significant revenue generation potential lies. For example:

The group $(0.154, 0.264]$ has a conversion rate of $20.02%$ and generates a high total revenue of $\$57,080$, with a revenue per customer of $\$2.00$. In contrast, the Percentile method only starts achieving similar conversion rates in later groups like $(0.202, 0.244]$, but with negative revenue due to an imbalance in customer distribution and inefficient segmentation.

Better Conversion Rate Progression

In the Optimized method, conversion rates progress more smoothly across the segments, ensuring that each group is targeted appropriately:

The Optimized methods conversion rate steadily increases from $9.12%$ to $100%$ as we move through the segments, showing a clear distinction between low and high-value customers. Meanwhile, the Uniform and Percentile methods have less consistent conversion rate progressions, making it harder to isolate high-converting customers early in the segmentation process. For example, the Uniform method has conversion rates as low as $0.06%$ in the first group, while the Percentile method starts as low as $0.038%$, both leading to poor revenue optimization.

Revenue Efficiency

The Optimized method achieves far better revenue per customer in every segment, demonstrating that its not just about increasing conversions but also about extracting maximum value from each customer:

The highest segment in the Optimized method reaches a revenue per customer of $\$15.23$, whereas the Uniform method maxes out at $\$9.24$ in its best-performing segment. The Percentile method struggles the most, with negative revenue in the majority of its groups and only a modest recovery towards the end.
